## Overview

I am interested in the circulation of the Arctic Ocean and its variability. I use a combination of ocean models, state estimates, observations, and occasionally theory, to investigate the dynamics of the Arctic Ocean. For my PhD, I am focusing on the salinity trends and variabilty of the Beaufort Gyre. 

## Current Projects

### Project 1: Beaufort Gyre Freshening, 2003-2016
![Salinity equation terms](/assets/images/salinity_equation_terms.png){: .align-center}
*Figure 1: Salinity equation terms for the upper 300m of the Beaufort Gyre, calculated in ECCOv4r4. The different terms show contributions of different mechanisms to freshening in the Beaufort Gyre.*
{: .text-center}

**Description:** The Arctic Ocean’s Beaufort Gyre has freshened over recent decades. Ocean models suggest that Beaufort Gyre freshwater can be exported to the North Atlantic and may modify the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation, with potential consequences for global climate. A mechanistic understanding of past Beaufort Gyre freshening is essential for anticipating future freshwater accumulation and release. Using ECCOv4r4, a dynamically-consistent, data-constrained ocean model, we construct a salinity budget for the upper 300m of the Beaufort Gyre between 1992-2017, and use Lagrangian particle tracking experiments to identify the origins of salinity changes. We find that the Beaufort Gyre freshened between 2003-2016, driven by two distinct mechanisms operating in sequence. From 2003 to 2008, freshening was caused by anomalous advection of relatively fresh water, originating primarily from the East Siberian and Laptev Seas. From 2010, warmer, saltier Atlantic water was drawn into the Beaufort Gyre from deeper than 300m, caused by increased ocean surface stress parallel to the coasts of the Canada Basin, which led to Ekman-induced upwelling. Although this Atlantic inflow acted to salinify the gyre by advection, freshwater input from melting sea ice outweighed the salinifying effect of Atlantic inflow, producing a second phase of freshening between 2011 and 2016.

### Project 2: Upwelling in the Beaufort Gyre
![Salinity equation terms](/assets/images/salinity_2015_trajs.png){: .align-center}
*Figure 2: Some lagrangian particle tracks, with their salinity plotted along their trajectories. Particles are seeded in the Beaufort Gyre and tracked backwards in time for 5 years. Moving backwards in time, particles get saltier -- these particles are sourced from deep Atlantic Water under the Beaufort Gyre.*
{: .text-center}

Our research in project 1 showed that upwelling of deep, salty, warm, Atlantic Water into the Beaufort Gyre increased over the past decade. This is due to the melting of sea ice in the Arctic, which reduces the sea ice pack strength, making it more mobile and thus increasing momentum transfer from the atmosphere to the ocean. However, the upwelling of warm Atlantic Water into the shallow Beaufort Gyre will itself have impacts on sea ice melting, stratification, and salinity. We aim to investigate the impact of upwelling of warm, deep water on Beaufort Gyre variability using CMIP6 models and Lagrangian particle tracking. Stay tuned to find out what we learn!

**Collaborators:** [Thomas Haine](https://sites.krieger.jhu.edu/haine/), [Anand Gnanadesikan](https://pages.jh.edu/agnanad1/)
